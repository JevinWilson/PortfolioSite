The Electric Vehicle Specs Dataset (2025) provides a collection of specifications and performance metrics for modern EVs.

create table electric_vehicles_spec_stagging
like electric_vehicles_spec_2025;

insert electric_vehicles_spec_stagging
select *
from electric_vehicles_spec_2025;

Create Stagging Table

In this code snippet I created a copy of the table to edit and stage changes on to keep the original table/data safe. 


-- most variety of electric vehicles by brand
select brand, count(brand) as Number_of_electric_models
from electric_vehicles_spec_stagging
group by brand
order by Number_of_electric_models desc;

-- quickest EVs manufactured in 2025
select brand, model, acceleration_0_100_s, car_body_type
from electric_vehicles_spec_stagging
order by acceleration_0_100_s asc;

select 
    segment,
    count(*) as total_models,
    count(distinct brand) as brands_in_segment,
    round(avg(battery_capacity_kWh), 1) as avg_battery_size,
    round(avg(range_km), 0) as avg_range,
    group_concat(distinct brand order by brand) as brands_list
from electric_vehicles_spec_stagging
where segment is not null
group by segment
order by total_models desc;

How is the EV market structured across different segments?

Using the dataset we can see that "larger" vehicles dominate the most popular vehicle segment manufactured in 2025 with the top 5 being medium sized SUVs, larger sized SUVs, full sized luxury, passenger vans, and smaller sized SUVs. After the larger vehicles there is a noticable drop of almost 30% to the rest of the vehicle segments. But this doesn't tell the whole story of the most popular vehicles segments due to not having sales data. 


select drivetrain, count(*) as model_count, round(avg(efficiency_wh_per_km),0) as avg_efficiency,
round(avg(range_km)) as avg_range, round(avg(acceleration_0_100_s)) as avg_accel, 
round(avg(torque_nm),0) as avg_torque, round(avg(battery_capacity_kWh)) as battery_capacity
from electric_vehicles_spec_stagging
group by drivetrain
order by avg_efficiency asc;

How do different drivetrains compare in real life efficiency?

It is not surprising for FWD to be the most efficienct due to FWD having a history of being a cheaper consumer option with cheaper and lighter materials used. You would think that they would have the most range due to being the most efficienct but they have the lowest avg battery capacity. I think it is due to my point earlier that FWD vehicles are mainly mostly known to be consumer affordable options and manufactures cheap out on the battery for them. It is also no surprise to see FWD vehicles being the worse on all performance metrics due to its inferior weight transfer, handling/cornering, balance, etc, automaker don't tend to make FWD vehicles with performance in mind. 


with battery_efficiency as (
	select 
		case
			when battery_capacity_kWh < 50 then 'small'
            when battery_capacity_kWh < 75 then 'medium'
            when battery_capacity_kWh < 100 then 'large'
            else 'more than 100'
		end as battery_category,
        battery_capacity_kWh, efficiency_wh_per_km, range_km, car_body_type
	from electric_vehicles_spec_stagging
    where battery_capacity_kWh is not null and efficiency_wh_per_km is not null
)
select battery_category, count(*) as vehicle_count, round(avg(battery_capacity_kWh),1) as avg_battery_size,
round(avg(efficiency_wh_per_km)) as avg_efficiency, round(avg(range_km)) as avg_range
from battery_efficiency
group by battery_category
order by avg_battery_size;

Do bigger batteries mean less effective power units?

As the battery size increases the avgerage efficiency gets worse as well as the range. So yes, according to our dataset a bigger battery means less efficiency. I wouldn't say a less effective power unit because there are other metrics and factors not available in the dataset that would be needed. One of the main and obvious reason I can think of why this is, is simply because manufactures try to offset the lower efficiency with a larger battery capacity.
